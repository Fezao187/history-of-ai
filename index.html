What is AI?

Artificial Inteligence is a field of computer science focused on creating systems that can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence.
Such as:

  • Understanding language (natural language processing, chatbots, translation)
  • Learning from data (machine learning, pattern recognition)
  • Problem-solving and reasoning (decision-making, planning)
  • Perception (recognizing images, sounds, objects)
  • Interaction (robots, self-driving cars, recommendation systems)

Types of AI

  • Narrow AI: AI designed for a specific task (e.g., Siri, Google Translate, Netflix recommendations).
  • General AI: A more advanced form that could, in theory, perform any intellectual task a human can (this doesn’t exist yet).
  • Superintelligent AI: A hypothetical future AI that surpasses human intelligence in all areas.

1. The Dawn of Computing and AI's Birth (1939-1950)

1939: Alan Turing and the First Computer

  • The world faced technological warfare with the Nazis' Enigma machine, an "unbreakable" encryption device.
  • Alan Turing, a visionary mathematician, developed the first computer to break the Enigma code.
  • This machine became the bedrock for the modern Digital World, redefining warfare and starting the era of computing.
  • Turing questioned, "can machines think?" and was convinced they could and would perform human-like tasks.

1946-1947: The Need for Efficiency and the Semiconductor

  • By 1946, the military recognized computers' power but found them inefficient due to large vacuum tubes.
  • Computers were "the size of a warehouse" and required "manual labor to maintain".
  • In 1947, physicist Bill Shockley imagined powering computers with germanium to create a semiconductor, acting like an "electrical switch".
  • This became the transistor, promising to shrink computing power to "the size of a fingertip," fueling ideas of "super powerful computers exhibiting artificial intelligence".

1950: The Turing Test

  • Alan Turing introduced the Imitation Game, or Turing Test, to determine if a machine could "exhibit intelligent Behavior indistinguishable from that of a human".
  • This "fundamentally questioned the limits of artificial and human intelligence" and "etched" AI's power into the human psyche.
  • Top universities like Harvard, MIT, and Princeton began offering computer science degrees due to this fascination.

2. Early Neural Networks & Silicon Valley's Genesis (1950s-1960s)

1950s: Marvin Minsky and the First Neuronet

  • Inspired by Turing, Marvin Minsky, a 23-year-old PhD candidate at Princeton University, built the world's first working neuronet.
  • It used wires and "six vacuum tubes as the synapses," laying the "foundation for the field of artificial intelligence and the bay Rock that's the neural network".

1954-1956: Shockley Semiconductor and the "Traitorous Eight"

  • The success of the transistor radio led Bill Shockley to leave Bell Labs and start Shockley Semiconductor in Palo Alto in 1954.
  • He recruited 12 brilliant minds, including Gordon Moore and Robert Noyce.
  • In 1956, eight of these recruits, dubbed the "Traitorous Eight" by Shockley, defected to form Fairchild Semiconductor.
  • Fairchild quickly became profitable by selling 100 silicon-based transistors to IBM for the B70 bomber's navigation system, proving silicon's superiority over germanium.
  • Silicon-based transistors offered a 30 times larger profit margin.

Fairchild's Innovations: Planar Process and Integrated Circuit

  • John Hoerni, a physicist at Fairchild, invented the planar process, a more efficient manufacturing method that added a protective coating to transistors for "stability and durability". Fairchild held the profitable patent.
  • The industry faced a "stalemate" as transistors could only "do one thing at a time" [9].
  • Bob Noyce, leader of Fairchild, created the integrated circuit, allowing "transistors to operate together in unison like a network". (Note: Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments technically created it first, but Noyce's version was superior).
  • This led to a "mass Exodus" of Fairchild employees, forming the "Fairchildren" (e.g., LSI Logic, AMD, Intel), which today power "over 92% of the world's computing power".

1966: Eliza, the World's First Chatbot

  • Joseph Weizenbaum, an MIT computer scientist, created Eliza, the "world's first chatbot," on an IBM 794 computer.
  • Inspired by Minsky's neuronet, Eliza used "pattern matching and substitution" to simulate conversations, giving users "the illusion of understanding".

3. Microprocessors, Algorithms, and Deep Blue (1971-1997)

1971: The Microprocessor Revolution

  • Intel faced a challenge from Japanese company Busicom to build complex integrated circuits for a calculator.
  • Intel engineer Ted Hoff realized the solution was to build a "tiny computer itself" – the microprocessor.
  • This led to the 4004, the first microprocessor, making computers "small enough and cheap enough to sit on the desk".
  • This innovation paved the way for companies like Microsoft and Apple in the late 1970s, which "laid the groundwork for modern Computing" and the PC boom of the 80s and early 90s.

1986: Backpropagation Algorithm

  • British computer scientist Geoffrey Hinton created backpropagation, a "machine learning algorithm that acts like a teacher who helps the computer learn from his mistakes".
  • It checks answers and provides tips for improvement, instantly becoming the "cornerstone for all neuron Nets".

1997: IBM Deep Blue and the AI Formula

  • IBM Deep Blue, an AI program combining "sophisticated search algorithm, a database of games played, and combined it with brw Force computing power," defeated the world's best chess player.
  • This provided the "blueprint for the future of AI": combining "large volumes of data, strong algorithms and a lot of compute power" to build "super intelligent" systems.

4. The Information Age and the "Godfather of AI" (late 1990s-2006)

Late 1990s: The Information Age and Big Data

  • The rise of internet companies like Google, Amazon, and Facebook ushered in the information age.
  • These companies generated "hundreds of companies generating more data every year than all of humanity did in a century".
  • This created a demand for "new and innovative ways to analyze the data," drawing top computer scientists.

2006: Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

  • Jeffrey Hinton, now a professor at the University of Toronto, developed the convolutional neural network (CNN) algorithm.
  • CNNs allowed computers to "think, recognize patterns and even images".
  • Tech giants like Facebook (photo tagging), Google (photo recognition), Amazon (recommendations), and Apple (phone unlocking) adopted CNNs.
  • Hinton earned the title "Godfather of AI" for this "groundbreaking contribution".

5. The "Big Bang of AI" and Google's Rise (2007-2017)

2007: ImageNet Competition

  • Dr. Fei-Fei Li, a professor at Caltech, launched ImageNet, a competition offering "the world's largest open-source data set" to find the best algorithm for "true intelligence".

2012: AlexNet and the "Big Bang of AI"

  • Jeffrey Hinton and his students, Ilya Sutskever and Alex Krizhevsky, entered ImageNet with their CNN model, AlexNet.
  • This moment is called the "Big Bang of AI" or "Big Bang of deep learning," widely known as "the day that AI was born".
  • The success of AlexNet relied on the GPU computing power from Nvidia.
  • Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang shifted the company's focus from graphics cards to "building the most powerful microchips in the world" for AI.
  • This bet made Nvidia the third richest company globally, powering "92% of the world's AI".

2015: Google's AI Dominance

  • Google acquired DeepMind, an AI company, and hired top AI talent, including Hinton's team from DNN research.
  • By 2015, Google held "a complete Monopoly on the frontier AI model space".

2015: Birth of OpenAI

  • Concerned about a for-profit company monopolizing super-intelligent AI, Silicon Valley billionaires like Elon Musk, Reed Hoffman, and Peter Thiel founded OpenAI.
  • OpenAI aimed to be an "open source nonprofit company geared towards building AGI safely".
  • Elon Musk personally recruited Ilya Sutskever from Google, one of AlexNet's creators, to join OpenAI.

2016-2017: AlphaGo, AlphaZero, and Transformers

  • In 2016, Google's DeepMind developed AlphaGo, which beat the world's strongest Go player, followed by AlphaZero, a chess program that did the same.
  • In 2017, Google discovered Transformers, "super smart robots that can read a lot of books really fast," powerful AI models for processing vast text data.
  • This sparked the idea of combining Transformers with human training to create a better chatbot than Eliza.

6. The Age of Generative AI (2020-Present)

2020: OpenAI's Capital and Leadership Change

  • OpenAI needed "a minimum of $1 billion" to compete.
  • They brokered a deal with Microsoft for a "capital profits" in exchange for the investment.
  • This led Elon Musk to leave OpenAI due to its shift from open-source principles.
  • Sam Altman became the sole CEO of OpenAI.

2022: ChatGPT and Generative AI Boom

  • In 2020, with a $10 billion investment from Microsoft, OpenAI launched an "updated version of Eliza," the chatting Generative Pre-trained Transformer (ChatGPT), in 2022.
  • ChatGPT became the "fastest-growing app in history," reaching 100 million users within months.
  • The same year (2022) saw 11 Labs, Midjourney, and Stability AI launch groundbreaking generative AI tools for "audio and images," officially "sending the entire world into the age of AI".

Present: Continued Investment and Impact

  • Today, "over $40 billion a year is being invested in AI startups".
  • Nvidia and Microsoft are "two of the three richest companies in the world," validating their AI bets.
  • Startups like OpenAI are seeking "over $7 trillion in investment" for long-term AI and robotic production.
